# Killer Cover Letter — Principles (2026)

The single rule: **a cover letter is a business case, not a personal statement.**
The resume lists what you did; the letter answers what the resume can't —
**why you, why this company, why now.** 83% of hiring managers read cover letters;
45% read them *before* the resume. It is a real screen.

## Structure (250–400 words, three moves)

1. **HOOK (2–3 sentences) — fit + their problem.**
   State the company's core bet / problem space in one sentence, then assert the match.
   Get into the "yes" pile fast. NEVER open with "I am excited to apply" or a passion preamble.

2. **PROOF (1–2 short paragraphs) — Problem → Solution → Impact.**
   ONE story that maps to their need. State the problem, your concrete method, and a
   **measurable result**. Extend a resume line into a story; do not summarize the resume.

3. **FIT / WHY-NOW (2–3 sentences).**
   Show you researched them (a real, recent fact), tie your trajectory to where they're
   going, close with quiet confidence. End on what the company needs, not what you want.

## What makes it killer (the differentiators)
- **Specific company research.** One genuine recent detail about *them* beats a paragraph of passion. Strongest "serious applicant" signal.
- **A metric only you could write.** Numbers + scale = instant credibility.
- **Evidence of shipping and learning, not fake seniority.** A student's edge: "I build real things real users touch."
- **Sounds like a person.** Genuine intent, plain language, one human sentence about why the problem interests you.

## Instant-reject AI-tells (strip all of these)
- "I am writing to express my interest…", "I am excited to apply", "proven track record", "detail-oriented", "passionate about technology".
- Restating the resume instead of extending it.
- Over-polished, flawless-but-empty prose (reads machine-made).
- Focusing on what *you'd* get ("great learning opportunity for me") instead of what *they* need.
- Zero company-specific reference; generic to any employer.

## Length & format
- 350–400 words is the sweet spot; under 250 reads thin, over 500 reads indulgent. One page.
- Hiring managers don't care about margins/fonts — they care whether you make a clear case, fast.

## Human-in-the-loop with AI
Use AI for structure/phrasing, then inject 3–4 **experience anchors** (real outcomes,
metrics, a personal line only this candidate could write). Keep the skeleton; replace
filler with lived specifics.

## Final quality checklist (must all pass)
- [ ] Opening says something specific about THIS company/role — not just excitement.
- [ ] At least one concrete achievement with a measurable result.
- [ ] Answers "why you, why this company, why now" — not just a skills list.
- [ ] Reads like a real person wrote it with genuine intent.
- [ ] Under 400 words, one page.
- [ ] No AI-tell phrases; no resume restatement; no brackets/placeholders left.
